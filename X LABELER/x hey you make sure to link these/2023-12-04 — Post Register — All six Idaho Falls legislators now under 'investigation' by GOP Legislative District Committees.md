Idaho Falls Reps. [[Barbara Ehardt]], [[Marco Erickson]] and Sen. [[Dave Lent]] have been added to the list of state legislators called into hearings by Idaho GOP [[Legislative District 32]] and [[Legislative District 33|33]] [[central committees|committees]] for alleged violations of the Idaho GOP platform.

Under the Idaho GOP’s new Republican Platform Enforcement rule, Legislative District Committees are “empowered to call into question the conduct of a Republican elected official; … determine if substantive violations of Party Platform or Constitutions have occurred; … and for any substantive violations may remove Party support and prohibit the use of Republican Party identifiers on campaign information and advertising from the elected official during their current term and any subsequent campaign for political office for a period of five (5) years.” The rule passed on June 22 during the Idaho GOP’s summer convention in [[Challis]].

Erickson said he received a 55-page packet from the [[Legislative District 33]] Committee several days before Thanksgiving with 14 allegations of platform violations.

He said he has received no communication from party officials about where or when the hearing will be held.

Lent was accused of seven allegations in a 38-page document — including votes in favor of [[Idaho Launch]] (S.B. 1167), appropriating funds to [[WWAMI]] joint medical education program (S.B. 1147), appropriating funds for [[state colleges|colleges and universities]] (S.B. 1176) and signing a petition calling for a special legislative session to create a May [[presidential primary]].

He said that for every vote he made that “allegedly violated the Idaho GOP platform” except one — the Launch bill — he voted with the majority of Republican legislators.

For the three additional accusations, Lent said, “They’re using the Idaho Freedom Foundation scoring table to judge me against the platform. … I really would have to say, is it the Republican party or is it the Freedom Foundation doing this?”

For Ehardt, the request to appear before a committee came as a surprise.

“I was genuinely shocked to learn of this,” she said in a written statement. “It appears that the main thing for which I’m being censored are my votes that were in favor of educational spending. For my constituents, education spending is a top priority. But even so, I have not gone against the Republican Party Platform or conservative principles.”

In a Friday statement, the Bonneville County GOP Central Committee encouraged the legislators to participate in the hearing process.

“The Bonneville County Republican Party strongly supports the right of citizens to petition their government officials — including citizens who make up political parties. … We thank elected officials who are willing to answer questions, address concerns, and stand behind their actions. We recognize the extreme importance of listening, discussion, and engagement with one another, even through rigorous questioning or when not all parties are in complete agreement.”

Bonneville GOP Party Chairman Nick Contos also clarified that the hearings are being held by District 32 and 33 Legislative District Committees, not by the Bonneville County GOP Central Committee. While each of Idaho’s 44 counties has a central committee, local GOP precinct committee officers are also organized in independent legislative district committees.

“There’s 35 legislative districts across Idaho,” Contos said. “Each legislative district forms a committee too, so it’s the same precinct officers but it’s a different body — kind of like a city and county. They overlap, but they’re not really the same thing.

“... Legislative committees are committees of the precinct officers in each legislative district. In the committee, that body is focused on their legislators — two House members and one Senator. … Both county committees and legislative district committees make up the Idaho State Republican Party.”

Erickson and Lent provided the Post Register copies of the petition and allegations made against them.

The packet contains eight pages of rules that will govern the Legislative District 33 Committee and Special Investigative Committee hearings process.

In an opening letter, Legislative District 33 Chairwoman Jilene Burger wrote the legislators, “I feel it is imperative to provide a platform to give fair consideration to alleged violations and provide a meaningful opportunity for the official to be heard, and determine if substantive violations of Party Platform or Constitutions have actually occurred.”

Burger will “establish an Investigative Committee (IC) of four District 33 precinct committee officers, elected by the committee and the Chair, and appointed by the Chairwoman. The IC will individually provide a non-confrontational and unbiased atmosphere for the petitioners and legislator to provide full and thorough information each to their satisfaction.”

The petition against Erickson criticizes his votes for Idaho Launch (H.B. 24), consolidating the March presidential primary election with May primary election (H.B. 138), a Rural Nursing Loan Repayment Program (H.B. 213), a bill “to require all 6-12 public schools and charter schools to provide feminine hygiene products” (H.B. 313), appropriating funds for WWAMI joint medical education program (S.B. 1147) and appropriating funds for colleges and universities (S.B. 1176). It also alleges he voted against “removing school bond and levy election dates in August and March” (H.B. 58), “prohibiting state government and its employees from paying membership fees or dues with public funds with some exemptions” (H.B. 91), allowing voters to “use an initiative or referendum to reduce the property tax base budget of a city or county” (H.B. 273), establishing state Board of Education member elections (H.B. 293) and the ‘Children’s School and Library Protection Act’ (H.B. 314).

“I’m not really giving a lot of attention to negativity, because I’m just focused on what we’re doing well. Usually when I get stuff like that, I just don’t care to engage with them,” Erickson said.

The Investigative Committee will determine whether or not allegations should be forwarded to a Legislative District Committee for a second hearing composed of District 33’s 17 precinct committee officers or their assistants.

The initial petitions are signed by Legislative District 33 Precinct Committee Officers Tim Urling (Precinct 16), Paul Voegeli (Precinct 19), Shara Zollinger (Precinct 4) and Ryan Hancock (Precinct 7).

Lent and Erickson said they do not plan to participate due to the lack of transparency on the committees’ votes and the committees’ control over how much time legislators are given to respond to allegations.

“When they go into executive session here, their actual voting is secret ballot, … so they don’t even know who’s voting on what,” Lent said. “… They’ll hand them a piece of paper, and give them a yes or no on that so that at no time are they actually accountable for their vote. … (Also) if I want more time to respond, they have to take a vote and decide whether or not they’re going to give me the time to respond. So they’re still controlling it.”

Contos said that the committees’ votes are confidential just like every voter’s ballot in a general election or legislators’ votes for the Speaker of the House.

“Secret ballot means that there’s nobody there coercing anybody or raising hands … so that nobody feels pressured into anything,” Contos said. “… It avoids probably both pressure beforehand and also retaliation afterwards.”

## **Legislative District 32 hearings**

The new Legislative District 33 Committee investigations add to the Legislative District 32 hearings underway. On Nov. 25, a Special Investigative Committee hearing was held for Reps. Wendy Horman, Stephanie Mickelsen and Sen. Kevin Cook at Smith Chevrolet.

All three legislators chose not to participate in person in the Special Investigative Committee hearing after Legislative District 32 Chairman Doyle Beck did not guarantee that the Special Investigative Committee member’s votes would be made in public.

Horman submitted a written response to the four alleged violations of the Idaho GOP platform that had been made against her in a petition signed by Bonneville County GOP precinct committee officers Carolyn Harrison, John Jensen, Rusty Devereaux and Lisa Keller.

Special Investigative Committee Chairwoman Barbara Miller, who recently lost her campaign for Idaho Falls City Council with 33.07% of the vote, wrote in a Nov. 29 email to Cook, “We were saddened to see that you chose not to attend. There were many questions that the committee members would have liked to asked you in order to better understand the bill evaluation process. In particular, the committee would like to hear about why you voted for SB-1147, SB-1176 and SB1167, with the issues being: increased government spending, bailouts/subsidies (launch program) of higher education and funding that is fungible (i.e. allows other funds to be freed up for these purposes) like critical race theory, [[abortion]] and LGBTQ activist organizations, etc. (WWAMI).

“With regard to HB 314 we would appreciate a response from you as to why you voted for the bill initially then voted against it following the Governor’s veto. We would very much appreciate understanding what changed your mind with respect to this bill.”

Miller asked Cook to respond in the next 30 hours.

In a statement to the Post Register, Cook said, “I do not plan to respond to them. I already spoke to the BCRCC along with all of the other legislators from LD 32, 33, 30 back in May regarding the exact same questions/bills.”

The full Legislative District 32 Committee hearing for Mickelsen, Horman and Cook will be held Tuesday at 7 p.m. at Smith Chevrolet.
