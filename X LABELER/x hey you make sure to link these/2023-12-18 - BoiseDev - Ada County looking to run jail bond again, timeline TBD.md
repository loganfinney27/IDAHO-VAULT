The Ada County Sheriff’s Office is not giving up after the attempt to expand its overcrowded jail via a bond narrowly [[2023-11-08 - BoiseDev - Bond to expand Ada County jail fails|failed in November]].

Representatives from ACSO, the Ada County Clerk’s Office, Operations, and the Prosecutor’s Office met with the Ada County Commissioners last week to discuss the recent failure of the $49 million jail expansion bond and a potential path forward. The bond received a 65.8% in-favor vote in November’s election, just shy of the needed 66.7%.

The public officials discussed rerunning the bond, strategies for moving forward, and both short and long-term solutions for the overcrowding the jail is currently facing. At the time of publication, the Ada County Jail had a total of 985 inmates. The jail has an operational capacity of 949. Of those 985, 21 inmates were sleeping in cots on the floor.

## Refloating the bond

The possibility of funding the jail expansion in a series of phases was discussed, but members of ACSO said it could cost taxpayers more in the long term with rising interest rates and construction costs, it would cause more disruptions to the jail to have construction extended out, and they faced the problem of delays in getting multiple bonds approved. Funding the jail expansion all at once would cause construction to take two years, doing it in phases could last almost 40 years, according to ACSO.

During the meeting, representatives from ACSO pushed to get a timeline for a possible bond refloat nailed down by January. The [[County Commissioners|commissioners]] said to do this, they would like to take a look at voter turnout analytics for both a May primary election and a November general election. Additionally, the county wants to assemble a group of people from the community who can campaign for the passage of the bond.

Having a pro-bond campaign was something the public officials felt was missing from the last attempt to fund the jail expansion. This was largely due to time constraints as the commissioners decided to float the bond just two months before the election. County officials are not allowed to campaign for the bond’s passage due to [[Idaho Code|Idaho State law]], so they are looking to recruit people from the area’s various chambers of commerce to do the campaigning for them.

## The issue of state inmates

In trying to decide how to alleviate the jail’s overcrowding in the short term, Commission Chair Rod Beck brought up the issue of the county jail housing state inmates well after sentencing. For example, Beck said he recently spoke with a state inmate in [[Ada County]]’s custody who was allegedly sentenced in February and is still waiting to be transferred to an Idaho Department of Corrections prison. Beck called this unacceptable. As of Dec. 14, the [[Ada County Jail]] had 85 state inmates who were ready to be transported to an [[Department of Correction|IDOC]] facility.

The county received $75 a day from the state for each [[State of Idaho|state]] inmate it houses, but Beck said this doesn’t cover the expense of housing them. When he brought up the idea of asking the state for more money to pressure the state into expediting the process of taking its inmates, [[County Sheriff|Sheriff]] [[Matt Clifford]] said getting more money would be a “tough sell” to the [[Idaho Legislature|Idaho State Legislature]].
