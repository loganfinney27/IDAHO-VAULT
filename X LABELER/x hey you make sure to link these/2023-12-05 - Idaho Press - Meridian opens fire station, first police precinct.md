[[Meridian]] opened its eighth [[fire station]] and first [[police precinct]] on Tuesday, an unthinkable development for many who started on the force decades ago. But Meridian has grown tremendously and likely will continue to expand in the northwestern part of the city.  
  
Long yellow lines stretched down the apparatus bay of Fire Station #8 Tuesday morning. Toward one end, a red fire truck and white ambulance faced the road and an empty field. Toward the other end, homes stood tall against the foothills where last year construction had been underway. 

“They go quick,” Meridian Fire Deputy Chief Charlie Butterfield said. “We try to get there as quick as we can to stop it. The quicker we can get to an event, the quicker we can mitigate it…having strategically located fire stations around helps with response times to emergencies.”  
  
The fire station has a public area up front, with chairs and a public restroom. There’s a glass screen looking out to desks where the firefighters can do things like report writing.  

Down the hallway, a door leads to a wide-open warehouse-like area — the apparatus bay. The bay can hold three fire engines, and the room is extra-deep in case the department wants to put a ladder truck in there.  
  
The fire department’s initial plan is to hold an engine, a brush truck and an ambulance in Fire Station #8.  
  
On the other side of the apparatus bay is a small gym with a black floor and wooden walls. There’s also a decontamination room with heavy-duty laundry machines and a shower. Next is a dedicated room for firefighters’ gear, jackets and boots in open-air red lockers.  
  
“A lot of the fires we go to, there’s a lot of carcinogenic material from burning homes,” Butterfield said. “Some of the things we get involved in, trauma, traumatic injuries, vomit, blood, biohazards…we want to be able to come back from those types of calls and have an area that can clean biohazards, we can clean carcinogenic materials away from where we live.”  

Next door is Meridian’s first [[police]] precinct.  
  
Lt. James Leslie started with Meridian police 25 years ago — when he estimated around 35 officers worked for the department. Now there’s almost 150, according to Meridian’s website.  
  
  
“I thought we’d always work out of one campus,” Leslie said. “That’s something that’s a first for us for sure.”  
  
The lobby opens up into two soft interview rooms — mainly intended for interviewing victims of crime. There’s a blue bench in one and a box of tissues. The rooms are all video and audio recorded. In the hallway behind the room, there’s a module that controls the camera recording, Leslie said. Officers can enter the police report number and the recording will be saved in the file for the officer.  
  
Next is a break area. Beyond that, facing the lobby, is the record reception area. People can come in and interact with a clerk and get what they need. Leslie estimates at full build out, around 30% of the patrol population would work here and 70% would still work out of the main campus.  
  
  
Further down are the report writing rooms with some offices for supervisory staff. There’s also an armory in this hallway for tools, equipment and firearms, though it was empty on Tuesday.  
  
There’s also an evidence processing room where officers will package evidence and place it in a locker controlled by technicians. But all the evidence placed here will ultimately be transferred back to the main campus.  
  
The precinct also has hard interview rooms for suspects, with hard gray floors, one table and two black chairs.  
  
“It’s a little more on the rigid side,” Leslie said. “It’s a lot less inviting.”  
  
The police will also have mailboxes, a briefing room, men’s and women’s locker rooms and a gym.  
  
“The Highway 16 extension, that’s going to create a tremendous amount of work for us on this side of the valley,” Leslie said. “It’s just easier to get there, you know, they can get there a little bit quicker, so it just improves our response time.”  
  
  
Shawn Rayne, chief of [[Ada County Paramedics]], said paramedics will be there when they can, when there are extra personnel. He said the paramedics will see during their spring budget process if they can afford to hire staff to be here permanently.  
  
There are two rooms in the firefighters’ quarters that are dedicated to the paramedics.  
  
Growth has affected the paramedics as well. When Rayne started almost 28 years ago, he said there were only five fire stations in [[Ada County]] and one for western Ada County. Fire Station #8 will be number 15.  
  
And Rayne said the average age of people moving in from out of state in 2021 was mid-60s.  
  
“When they move into a new subdivision, they come with their medical problems,” Rayne said. “We started seeing call volume that was a cornfield a year before.”
