The State of Idaho responded to a lawsuit over a decision to pull out of a planned sale of the [[Department of Transportation|ITD]] campus on State Street, but it came without the approval of top officials.

Last week, [[Attorney General]] [[Raúl Labrador]] filed a brief in response to a suit from developers Hawkins, Pacific Companies, and FJ Management alleging the state broke its own rules for getting rid of surplus property when the Idaho Legislature inserted language into two budget bills killing the sale of the campus.

Labrador filed his brief arguing that the state has the right to start a sale process, but isn’t required to go all the way through with it on May 15 on behalf of the [[State Board of Examiners|Board of Examiners]]. This three-member board is made up of [[Governor|Gov.]] [[Brad Little]], [[Secretary of State]] [[Phil McGrane]] and Labrador, and is charged with examining claims against the state.

But, despite the brief being on behalf of the [[State Board of Examiners|Board of Examiners]], the board never met beforehand to discuss the brief and approve Labrador’s filing in the case on their behalf. Meeting agenda records posted on the [[State Controller]]’s website show the last meeting the board had was April 16, ten days before Hawkins filed suit against Idaho.

“The governor has learned that a brief and answer were filed on behalf of the Board of Examiners in the lawsuit before the [[Idaho Supreme Court]],” Little’s spokesperson [[Madison Hardy]] told BoiseDev in an email. “The filings were neither presented to nor approved by the board prior to filing.”

This comes after Little declined to sign the budget bill for ITD over concern the language would disrupt the sale of the property. The Idaho Legislature also passed a bill earlier this year removing Labrador as the legal counsel for the Idaho Land Board and instead allowing the Department of Lands to hire its own lawyers for the board, hinting at possible concerns with Labrador serving as both a top-ranking elected official who votes on the board and a lawyer on behalf of the state.

Neither Labrador nor McGrane responded to questions about the brief prior to the publication of this story.

## No action on brief Tuesday
The case was discussed at Tuesday’s Board of Examiners meeting, but it’s unclear what the outcome of the meeting is.

The meeting was noticed on May 15 and an agenda was posted on May 16, but it was later amended on the 17th to add an executive session discussion related to the Hawkins lawsuit. The agenda also included space for the board to return and vote on something related to the suit, like pulling back the filed brief.

The board went into [[executive session]] to discuss the case and returned roughly half an hour later, but they opted not to take any action on the matter, and no one commented on the issue before adjourning.

It’s unclear how the board will proceed with its defense against the suit or if Labrador will continue to represent the Board of Examiners in the suit at all.
