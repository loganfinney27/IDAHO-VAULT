Like the rest of the [[Treasure Valley]], [[Emmett]] is drawing in new residents for its mountain setting and small-town feel.

But, you might not necessarily pick up on that by taking a stroll down the few blocks of Main Street near Washington Avenue.

Thirteen storefronts across 10 buildings in downtown Emmett are all owned by the same Boise company, [[Huckleberry Properties Idaho LLC]], according to public records obtained by BoiseDev. This company purchased all of the buildings at once in December of 2018 and has owned them ever since, leaving six spaces empty.

Emmett [[mayor]] [[Gordon Petrie]] told BoiseDev that situations like this are a downside to the constitutional right to own property and the city cannot make the owner rent the spaces to interested business owners or sell the buildings to someone who will, but he is still disappointed.

“It’s a ridiculous situation and the only thing we can do is to make sure the properties are safe and do not represent a hazard to anyone,” he said. “Regrettably I suppose, the fact that they may represent an economic hazard is not part of the equation.”

Huckleberry Properties Idaho LLC is registered to an office suite in a Parkcenter Boulevard office building, according to the address listed on paperwork for the properties obtained from the [[Gem County]] [[County Assessor|Assessor]] through a public records request. A search for the company on the Idaho [[Secretary of State]]’s website did not turn up any business incorporation paperwork for the company. The office building is owned by [[Lindaro of Idaho LLC]], which also did not show any business incorporation paperwork on the Idaho Secretary of State’s website.

Both Huckleberry and Lindaro are registered in the state of [[Delaware]], using a third-party registration service.

BoiseDev visited the office building to locate the office suite and the lobby of the building had a receptionist for furniture company [[Scandinavian Designs]]. Lindaro LLC’s property tax information is sent to the Scandinavian Designs store on Broadway Ave. A message left with the receptionist with our contact information and asking about the Emmett properties was not returned prior to the publication of this story.

Only one tenant of the buildings agreed to do an interview with BoiseDev.

## Where are the properties?
Huckleberry Properties’ holdings are on both sides of Main Street in downtown Emmett.

The company owns 107 E Main Street, which is the office of the Gem County Recreation District, and 117-119 E Main Street, where computer store [[Computaters]] and [[People’s Financial Services]] are located. Huckleberry also owns the two buildings next door, 123 E. Main Street, where the now-closed Possum Trot Traders pawn shop is located, and the building addressed 125 E Main Street and 127 E Main Street. One storefront is empty, and the other is home to High Rock Law.

On the other side of the intersection of Washington Street, Huckleberry Properties owns the empty 122 W. Main Street, the western half of the same building with [[Hebrews Coffee]]. The empty storefronts at 132 and 136 W Main Street are also controlled by the company.

Huckleberry owns both buildings, home to home decore shops [[Hen House Home and Gift]] and [[The Barn Door]], located across the street from each other at the end of the block. 131 W Main Street, which used to be home to specialty candy store [[Sweet Corner of Idaho]] and is owned by Huckleberry, is now an extended part of Hen House. The empty spot at 142 W Main Street, on the end of block next to the Barn Door, is also owned by Huckleberry.

## Not the first out-of-town owner
Petrie said this isn’t the first time [[Emmett]] watched these buildings not be rented out.

He said Huckleberry Properties brought the slate of buildings from “another absentee landlord” and the city had high hopes for their redevelopment and new vibrancy in downtown, but it hasn’t happened. Petrie said this is likely due to the “perfect storm” of the timing where Huckleberry bought the properties at the end of 2018, a little more than a year before the state faced restrictions on business operations due to [[coronavirus|COVID-19]].

“We were having issues with the prior owner and we were very happy when the new guy came in because he was going to make Main Street pop again like it was in the 50s,” Petrie said. “So far that has not come to fruition.”

The prior owner of the buildings listed on paperwork from the Gem County Assessor’s Office is a now-defunct Payette River LLC, which was formed in Bend, [[Oregon]] and last had an address in East Wenatchee, [[Washington]] according to the paperwork on the Idaho Secretary of State’s website. All but three of the buildings were purchased by Kenneth L Buzard in 2006 and 2007 from different individual owners before being transferred eventually to Payette River LLC. Buzard’s signature is on a change of registered agent form filed for Payette River LLC in 2009, linking him to the company. He died earlier this year in Wenatchee.

[[Forrest Tevbaugh]], former owner of [[Possum Trot Traders]], said he’s rented his space since 1985 for his antique gun business. He said representatives from Huckleberry Properties talked with business owners on Main Street a few years ago, but other than that he doesn’t have a relationship with the owner, but he says his building is in “reasonably good shape.”

He thinks the owners would rent these spaces if they could, but it’s been on the decline anyway due to traffic through [[Emmett]] passing south of town on [[Highway 16]] and [[Highway 52]].

“All the businesses have pulled out of here pretty much,” he said. “The area is sort of degrading on its own because there’s no active business here.”

Petrie said the owner of Huckleberry Properties, who was not identified by name on any of the paperwork [[BoiseDev]] could locate, is “very difficult” to get in touch with, and he knows of at least one tenant who used to rent one of the spaces on the western end of Main Street who had water leakage issues and left the building.

“It’s sad, but this is what [[Emmett]] has been left with,” Petrie said. “There were local people who owned (the buildings) at one time. They have long since passed and speculators came who have a little bit of money and are able to pick them up and sometimes they fix things up and sometimes they don’t.”
