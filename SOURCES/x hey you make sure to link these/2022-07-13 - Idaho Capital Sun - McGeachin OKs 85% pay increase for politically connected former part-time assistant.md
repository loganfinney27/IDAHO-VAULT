### *Two Idaho lieutenant governor candidates running to succeed McGeachin question her budget decisions*

The day after she narrowly avoided running a deficit in her office budget, outgoing Idaho Lt. Gov. [[Janice McGeachin]] gave a large pay increase to a politically connected temporary, part-time administrative assistant, public records show.

Effective July 1, which was the first day of the state’s new [[Fiscal Year 2023|2023 fiscal year]], McGeachin hired [[Idaho Republican Party]] Vice Chair [[Machele Hamilton]] to serve as McGeachin’s new director of strategy and constituent services, according to Idaho [[Division of Human Resources]] records and an email that McGeachin sent Monday that the [[Idaho Capital Sun]] obtained. 

McGeachin ran unsuccessfully for governor in May’s Republican primary election.

Beginning Jan. 10, Hamilton had previously served as a temporary, part-time administrative assistant in McGeachin’s office. Under the temporary, part-time administrative assistant role, Hamilton was paid $20 per hour, Idaho Division of Human Resources records show. The new full-time position increased Hamilton’s pay to $37 per hour, which is about $77,000 per year**,** state records show. 

[The Associated Press](https://apnews.com/article/2022-midterm-elections-idaho-government-and-politics-0ce485bf410c4305f2daad394bc3ce5c) first reported on McGeachin authorizing Hamilton’s pay increase on Tuesday. 

The pay increase McGeachin approved for Hamilton is not illegal, but the 85% pay increase is significantly larger than the 7% increase the Idaho Legislature approved for state employees in the 2023 budget. 

Under the new pay increase, Hamilton will make considerably more than her boss, McGeachin, whose salary is $48,405 and is set [in [[Idaho Code|state law]]](https://legislature.idaho.gov/statutesrules/idstat/title59/t59ch5/sect59-501/) at 35% of the governor’s salary. Hamilton will also make considerably more than the previously highest paid employee in McGeachin’s office, former chief of staff [[Jordan Watters]], who made $22.81 per hour before Watters’ employment ended March 22, state records show.

McGeachin could not be reached for comment. Since April 4, McGeachin has not responded to more than a dozen telephone and email messages the Capital Sun has left.

#### **How does the pay increase affect Lt. Gov. McGeachin’s successor?**

Because she is paid more than the previously highest paid employee in the lieutenant governor’s office, the pay increase for Hamilton could also affect the budget for Idaho’s next lieutenant governor. That position is up for election in November – midway through the current [[fiscal year]]. 

“This is a perfect example of why we need change in Idaho, and this is exactly why I am running,” Democratic candidate for [[lieutenant governor]] [[Terri Pickens Manweiler]] told the [[Idaho Capital Sun]]. “(McGeachin) knows what she is doing, which is essentially giving the vice chair of the Republican Party a kickback for supporting her through her primary, in my opinion.”

“These guys are so insulated from their bad behavior, and that is why we need to break the supermajority and elect a Democrat who knows what it is like to run a small business,” Pickens Manweiler added. 

Speaker of the Idaho House [[Scott Bedke]], the Republican nominee running for lieutenant governor in November, issued the Sun a written statement about McGeachin approving the pay increase.

“I have always made it my mission to live within the Idaho taxpayers’ means,” Bedke wrote. “Not only is this mission ethically sound, but it’s constitutionally mandated. I can assure the people of Idaho that this type of questionable spending will not happen if I have the honor of being elected lieutenant governor. My office will focus on fiscal responsibility, transparency and helping advance conservative Idaho values.”

While running for higher office, McGeachin specifically made a campaign promise to “restrict the growth of local budgets” as part of her [10-point conservative vision](https://janiceforidaho.com/vision/). 

After losing the gubernatorial primary election, McGeachin will be leaving office in January when her successor is sworn in. 

Traditionally, incoming elected officials hire their own chief of staff or director of strategy and constituent services.

#### **McGeachin approved pay increase shortly after her own pay was deferred because of budget concerns**

The timing of McGeachin approving Hamilton’s pay increase is important. 

State officials withheld and deferred most of one of McGeachin’s paychecks in June because she was [projected to run a budget deficit](https://idahocapitalsun.com/2022/04/05/state-may-withhold-lt-gov-janice-mcgeachins-salary-if-she-doesnt-fix-budget-shortfall/) for the [[Fiscal Year 2022|2022 fiscal year]] if the state did not intervene and reduce expenses. As a result, McGeachin took home a paycheck of just $20.20 on June 24 to avoid a budget shortfall, [the Sun previously reported](https://idahocapitalsun.com/2022/07/01/idaho-poised-for-another-record-state-budget-surplus/). 

The 2022 fiscal year ended June 30, and the new 2023 fiscal year and 2023 state budget kicked in on July 1, the day McGeachin made Hamilton’s pay increase effective. State officials are deferring $1,713.26 from McGeachin’s June 24 paycheck until Aug. 5. But when state officials outlined the plan to defer McGeachin’s salary into the new fiscal year, they decided to wait until August to make her salary whole to allow salary savings to build up because the deferred part of McGeachin’s salary and her regular salary for the rest of her term in office all would be coming out of the 2023 budget.

“This will ensure sufficient salary savings have been accrued to keep the office personnel budget whole through the fiscal year, and it will keep the office in compliance with the annual pay for the elected position,” chief deputy state controller Joshua Whitworth wrote in a June 13 email to McGeachin.

Whitworth wrote that email and performed the accrued salary savings calculations before McGeachin announced she hired Hamilton at the increased pay levels. 

Now, Pickens Manweiler warns that McGeachin could spend down nearly half of lieutenant governor’s office budget before the next lieutenant governor takes office simply by paying the salaries and benefits for McGeachin and Hamilton.

An email McGeachin wrote Monday that was obtained by the Sun indicated Hamilton declined health insurance as a benefit.

New records obtained by the Capital Sun also show that McGeachin did not inform the Idaho [[Division of Financial Management]] of Hamilton’s new position and pay increase until 10 days after Hamilton’s start date. McGeachin first notified the Division of Financial Management about Hamilton’s new position and pay increase in an email McGeachin sent Monday. On Tuesday, McGeachin sent a follow up email with additional information.

“I neglected to state the effective date of July 1, 2022 for [[Machele Hamilton]]’s employment,” McGeachin wrote to deputy administrator [[David Fulkerson]] on Tuesday. 

#### **McGeachin’s budget problems began after she refused to release public records**

McGeachin originally ran into budget problems with her 2022 office budget after a district judge ruled McGeachin wrongfully withheld public records related to her 2021 education task force and ordered McGeachin to pay the Idaho Press Club almost $29,000 in legal costs and fees. After McGeachin withheld records requested by the Capital Sun and other Idaho news organizations, the Idaho Press Club filed suit seeking release of the records and won. 

McGeachin originally tried to get taxpayers to pick up the tab for her legal fees, but the Idaho Legislature’s Joint Finance-Appropriations Committee [declined to act](https://idahocapitalsun.com/2022/03/11/idaho-legislatures-budget-panel-oks-mcgeachin-budget-with-her-legal-costs-in-limbo/) on McGeachin’s $29,000 supplemental funding request during the 2022 legislative session. 

After McGeachin failed to secure supplemental funding, state officials took several steps to help her avoid a budget shortfall in the 2022 fiscal year, including pausing vendor payments and deferring McGeachin’s salary and benefits. Additionally, McGeachin worked without any paid staff for a period earlier this year to avoid a shortfall. 

After all of those steps were taken, McGeachin closed out the 2022 fiscal year with a balance of 72 cents in her budget, the Capital Sun previously reported. Meanwhile, the state of Idaho as a whole ended the 2022 fiscal year with another record state budget surplus, [most recently projected](https://idahocapitalsun.com/2022/07/01/idaho-poised-for-another-record-state-budget-surplus/) at $1.3 billion.
