Two state agencies involved in the lawsuit over whether it was legal for the [[Idaho Legislature]] to end the planned sale of the State Street [[Department of Transportation|ITD]] campus will now have their own lawyer, instead of relying on the [[Attorney General]].

This shakeup came in court filings on Wednesday after [[Attorney General|AG]] [[Raúl Labrador]] filed a brief in the case on behalf of the three-member [[State Board of Examiners|Board of Examiners]], which hadn’t met to sign off on his brief before it was put before the [[Idaho Supreme Court]].

Now, the [[Department of Transportation|Idaho Transportation Department]] and the [[Department of Administration]] hired [[Joan Callahan]] from [[Boise]] firm [[Naylor & Hayes]] to represent them in the case brought by the development group that sought to buy the 44-acre State Street property. A filing in the suit, brought by Hawkins, Pacific Companies, and FJ Management, said Callahan would defend the agencies instead of Labrador.

[[Secretary of State]] [[Phil McGrane]] also provided a statement Thursday, noting his opposition to the brief Labrador filed on behalf of the [[State Board of Examiners|Board of Examiners]]. Little told [[BoiseDev]] Tuesday that the brief wasn’t filed with his approval.

“As a member of the Board of Examiners, Secretary McGrane did not become aware of the suit until after the filing had been made,” McGrane’s spokesperson Chelsea Carattini said in an email. “He is hopeful that the Board of Examiners will be removed from the suit going forward.”

The case is expected to be heard mid to late August after filings pushing for an expedited timeline due to state [[budget|budgets]], which have language in them to cancel the sale, are due to take effect this summer.

## New lawyer, new arguments

This brief takes a different tact than Labrador’s.

In the AG’s filing, Labrador argued that the state had the right to start selling any state property but didn’t have to complete the sale, and the developers didn’t have a claim that the state legislature violated its own policies by stopping it at the last minute.

Callahan’s brief for ITD and the Department of Administration instead asks the [[Idaho Supreme Court]] to clarify whether a sale that goes through the state’s surplus property process is complete when the terms of sale are agreed upon, or if the legislature is allowed to intervene later, like it did during the [[2024 legislative session]].

“(The) Idaho Transportation Department needs clarity and direction about whether it has custody and control over the ITD Campus and whether it needs to engage in the necessary planning and process to rehabilitate the Phillip E. Batt Building on the ITD Campus,” the brief said.

Currently, the state’s statute governing how property is sold requires it to be declared surplus, given to the Board of Examiners and then the Department of Administration has the authority to sell it. Callahan’s filing says this is incongruous with the language in the two budget bills for ITD and the Department of Administration that are meant to decide on an annual basis how much money the state will spend on certain priorities.

And if this issue isn’t resolved by next year, it could trigger another fight after the 2025 budget bills aren’t valid anymore once the fiscal year flips to 2026.

The filing said the [[Idaho Supreme Court]] needs to decide if this bill violates the [[Idaho Constitution]]’s rules requiring bills to address a single subject, which aims to avoid problems that can arise when bills have multiple issues wrapped up in one piece of legislation. The brief also asks the court to decide, if the budget bills are unconstitutional, how the “urgent constitutional questions” need to be resolved.

“Without such a determination, ([[Department of Transportation|ITD]] and the [[Department of Administration]]) are left to contend with uncertain, incongruous, and revolving legal mandates,” the brief said.
