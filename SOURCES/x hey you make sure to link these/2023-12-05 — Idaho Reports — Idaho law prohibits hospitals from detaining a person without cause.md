After a [[Bonneville County]] [[County Sheriff|Sheriff]]’s deputy interacted with [[Jeremy Best]] on Nov. 30, and the following day he allegedly [[murder|murdered]] his wife and unborn child, some questioned why he wasn’t detained.  

The answer is simple. [[State of Idaho|Idaho]] law and the [[U.S. Constitution]].  

On Nov. 30, the Bonneville County Sheriff’s Office “responded to a disturbance at the [[Swan Valley]] General Store regarding a male who was nude, walking through the store. Deputies contacted the male who was identified as [[Jeremy Best]]. Mr. Best was evaluated by [[emergency medical services|EMS]] on scene and transported to [[Eastern Idaho Regional Medical Center]] by [[ambulances|ambulance]]. A [[Bonneville County]] deputy followed the ambulance and Mr. Best was turned over to the [[hospitals|hospital]] staff for further medical and mental health concerns,” according to a press release. 

Best is accused of killing his pregnant wife, [[Kali Jean Randall]], in [[Victor]], Idaho, early on Dec. 1. He’s accused of taking their 10-month-old son, [[Zeke Best|Zeke]]. After issuing an [[Amber Alert]] for the infant, authorities found Zeke dead. 

EIRMC officials confirmed that the hospital discharged Best from the hospital Nov. 30. [[Coleen Niemann]], an EIRMC spokesperson, said federal law prohibited her from disclosing specific information about any patient. 

“We are heartbroken by the tragedy that has rocked our community in the recent days and we join many in our area in extending our deepest sympathy to the families and loved ones of Kali and Zeke,” Niemann said Tuesday in an email. 

“Like all hospitals, we follow specific standards of care when we assess and treat individuals, including those with behavioral health concerns,” Niemann said. “Idaho law limits [[law enforcement]], [[hospitals]], and [[health care|healthcare]] providers from placing individuals on a [[protective custody hold]] unless specific clinical criteria is met. When these factors are not present or apparent at the time of the care process, hospitals cannot legally hold a patient against their or their family’s wishes.” 

[[Idaho Code]] 66-326 does allow for a 24-hour hold, but only if the “patient is mentally ill and either likely to injure himself or others or is gravely disabled due to mental illness.”  

If the patient does not meet that standard, the hold cannot apply. Additionally, Idaho law prohibits the placement of a hold on a patient who is “impaired by chronic alcoholism or drug abuse.” So, had Best been under the influence of a substance, that also cannot be a reason to incarcerate or hold a person at the hospital.  

Best does not have any charges filed related to his suspected public nudity, and at the time of the interaction with law enforcement at the [[Swan Valley]] General Store, he had not yet been charged with a crime.  

The Fourth Amendment of the [[U.S. Constitution]] protects people against unreasonable searches and seizures without probable cause.  

Knowing only what’s been made public in the case so far, deputies may not have had a legal reason to detain Best on Nov. 30. 

Best now remains in custody without bond on two counts of suspected first-degree murder and a weapons enhancement. His next court date is set for 9:30 a.m. Dec. 19 in [[Teton County]].
