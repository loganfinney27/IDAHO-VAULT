[[Department of Water Resources]] Meeting

PETER YOUNGBLOOD, USDA – [peter.youngblood@usda.gov](mailto:peter.youngblood@usda.gov) – 208-871-1245

Beginning of the year record low snowpacks

Near normal melt timing

April - North of Salmon River below average streamflow - South above average streamflow

May – Cooler and more snow – Slowed runoff

September – Clearwater got above average snowfall and that helped bolster streamflow

North Idaho only two of the last ten years saw normal snowpack

ROBIN FOX, NWS SPOKANE

Reached D3 ‘Extreme Drought’

Most deficits 1 inch or less. Central panhandle saw over 8 inch deficit on higher elevations.

Priest River was just under an inch deficit with close to 32 inches of precipitation. 12 inches of rainfall at Lewiston and under an inch deficit. Moscow 22.5 inches, 5 inch deficit.

Precipitation bump further north later in season.

Wet conditions and mud in Boundary and Bonner delayed planting

Palouse winter wheat received early moisture which helped roots establish

Central to South – less area to graze (feedings 3-4 weeks early), creeks and ponds dry early

Enough lake water for recreation season

No floods during spring runoff season!!

Lack of snowpack was initially a concern for recreation and ski season impacts

Reduced runoff and stream flows hampered supply for agriculture and livestock

JEREMY DALLING, BUREAU OF RECLAMATION

Snake River federal reservoirs above King Hill

Target Zero Flow at Milner to optimize storage– Idaho Power generates and returns water

Reservoir system functions to store excess winter/spring flows for delivery in summer/fall

Lower systems close to where they were last year, upper system slightly lower

With healthy carryover and runoff, Upper Snake reached nearly 100% full reservoirs – despite low natural flow – water use left reservoirs at slightly lower levels than WY2024
