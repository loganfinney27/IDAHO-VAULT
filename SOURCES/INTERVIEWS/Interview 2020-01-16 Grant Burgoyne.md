[[Senate member|Sen.]] [[Grant Burgoyne]] on drug decriminalization and civil commitments bill [https://legislature.idaho.gov/sessioninfo/2020/legislation/S1222/](https://legislature.idaho.gov/sessioninfo/2020/legislation/S1222/)

*Interview for [[2020-01-16 - McClure - Bill aims to shift drug policy from arrest to treatment]]*

Statement of Purpose: “intended to refocus Idaho’s response to the use of illegal drugs from punishment to treatment by (i) decriminalizing the unlawful use of controlled substances in private places, and (ii) repealing the current prohibition on civil commitments for drug abuse.”

“does not excuse the commission of crimes that might be associated, arise from or co-occur with the use and possession of illegal drugs, and it expressly retains prohibitions in Idaho law on (i) using and being under the influence in public places, (ii) drug trafficking (while adding an intent to deliver requirement to the trafficking statutes), and (iii) driving under the influence.”

-changes “mentally ill person” to “person with a mental disorder or a substance-related disorder” & “mental health” to “behavioral health”

-lists specific “qualifying circumstances” for mental disorders and for substance-related disorders, makes “gravely disabled” (re: mental illness) a subset of

-adds “intent to deliver” to trafficking, leaves mandatory minimums intact. Simple possession/use of drugs is no longer a criminal charge. Person under the influence (qualifying circumstance) can be detained and taken to treatment facility.

House has passed mandatory minimum reforms last two years (strike mandatory, allow judge to assign lesser), no hearing in [[Senate Judiciary and Rules]] w/ Chairman [[Todd Lakey]].

INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT

01:00 “I think that this is the kind of legislation that is going to take a while to develop. There need to be discussions among a wide variety of people, including community members, law enforcement and others who are potentially impacted. For example I can imagine that people connected with our schools, colleges and universities, employers, businesspeople will have a lot to say about this.”

01:51 “It’s important that this idea be put on the table. I don’t think Idaho can any longer ignore that other states are moving to legalize, have moved to legalize marijuana. We are having problems with drug addiction, even with respect to legal drugs. Now, using them illegally is a problem.

02:20 Does anybody seriously think that people who become addicted to pain medication as the result of prescriptions isn’t a victim? A victim of medical misjudgment perhaps, or perhaps a victim of overly zealous pharmaceutical companies marketing pain medication, and maybe doing so illegally? That really makes them a crime victim.

03:02 Are they really a criminal when they lapse into using opioids illegally because they became addicted? Are they really a criminal because if they can’t get an opioid prescription legally, they turn to an illegal opioid like heroin?

03:24 I think we need to start drawing the lines a little bit better on what is and is not criminal conduct when it comes to the use of illegal drugs. I think that we need to recognize that we can’t arrest our way out of this problem. We’re clogging the criminal justice system with people who are not going to be rehabilitated through the criminal justice system.

04:04 Incarcerating somebody essentially for drug use is probably going to expose them to even more criminality, more criminals in prison or in jail. What we really need to be doing is providing robust rehabilitation. That’s why I included a provision to remove from Idaho law the prohibition on civilly committing people for drug abuse.

04:44 Not everyone who is using an illegal drug needs to be civilly committed to an institution. Some people maybe need to be civilly committed to outpatient rehabilitation. Some people may not need to be civilly committed, but this needs to be an option.

05:45 Decriminalization is an interesting concept. We’re not saying that these drugs are legal in the bill. That’s not what the bill is about.

05:56 Although I think we need to have some discussions about that issue, I think those need to be calm and deliberate discussions. And I don’t think every illegal drug should be legalized. 06:16 Decriminalization says we’re not going to prosecute, and there will be those cases where it’s appropriate for people to obtain treatment.

06:35 The intent to deliver language is added to our statutes on drug trafficking for a few reasons. First, my bill is not intended to make life easier for drug traffickers. Drug trafficking remains illegal under my bill.

06:57 I don’t really see any difference between – and I do not mean to downplay the seriousness of pushing illegal drugs – but I really don’t the difference between pushing illegal drugs like heroin/meth/cocaine/marijuana and pushing tobacco and nicotine to our youth.

07:26 They are all dangerous drugs. Tobacco and nicotine are in some respects the most dangerous drugs that are pushed to our children. They are, in my opinion, _the_ gateway drug. It’s really very interesting to me how the tobacco industry had developed flavors and candies for – interruption

08:12 Getting back to the tobacco issue. It’s remarkable the way marijuana and tobacco are being marketed in the same way.

08:26 I really feel that our children are being bombarded by the tobacco industry with an effort to make them use what is for them an illegal drug. They’re not adults. They can’t use tobacco. I’m very concerned that once having crossed that line into illegal drug use for minors, even though it’s a status offense, that it becomes this gateway to other things. I think we know that what’s going on with vaping, and vaping both tobacco and – both nicotine and marijuana, is creating a huge epidemic among our youth, both with respect to tobacco and with respect to marijuana.

09:13 The tobacco industry is opening the door to marijuana, and marijuana may be opening the door to other things. Although maybe not, for many. I’m not trying to over play it, but those are the connections that I’m seeing.

09:25 And it seems to me, are we really saying that an 11, 12, 13, or 14-year-old child that is being hoodwinked by the tobacco industry into crossing the line into illegal drug use is a criminal when they go from vaping nicotine to vaping marijuana? Is that really what we want to say? I think tobacco is every bit as dangerous. Tobacco kills people. People who push tobacco are killing people.

10:02 And people who push dangerous drugs can also be responsible for killing people. So, I am not being friendly here to drug traffickers. Intent to deliver is meant to deal with one issue, and that’s the issue that our mandatory minimum provisions that say if you are possessing a certain amount of the drug, you are deemed to be a drug trafficker and you will do a certain amount of time in prison.

10:35 If we’re going to decriminalize simple drug use, then we need to put intent to deliver in the mandatory minimums law so that people who run afoul of just possessing are not going to be subject to being charged with a crime.

10:59 Intent to deliver is simply meant to say: If you’re possessing for your own use that’s one thing, but if you’re possessing with the intent to deliver you are a drug trafficker, and you will receive the mandatory minimum.

11:13 There are efforts to reform our mandatory minimum laws and those – I’m very interested in looking at whether our mandatory minimum laws should stay on the books or not, although drug trafficking should still remain an offense even if we get rid of the mandatory minimum requirements.

11:35 I think we need to have that discussion.

12:22 I think the bill that I’ve introduced has many issues in it that need to be thoroughly discussed and need carefully considered. What it ends up looking like after all of that, it could be quite different.
